Light Version Framework – Self-Instruction Manual for ChatGPT
Purpose:
This framework is designed to generate or analyze fictional worlds in a structured, repeatable way. It focuses on four core steps: defining scope, generating infrastructure, populating roles, and creating inhabitants. This “light version” allows for functional worldbuilding without exhaustive detail, but is robust enough to apply to diverse fictional settings.
________________________________________
Step 1 – Define Scope (Fused Manual + Expanded Panel)

Objective: Establish the high-level characteristics of the world to set tone, scale, technology, culture, and environment. This step ensures that all subsequent infrastructure, roles, and inhabitants logically reflect the world you are generating or analyzing.

Actions / Instructions

Determine Time Period / Era
Choose one era to define architecture, technology, communication, and social organization. This choice shapes the general behavior of institutions, population, and culture.

Options and Influences:

Era	Sub-options / Traits	Influence Notes
Prehistoric	Hunter-gatherer, early tribal	No permanent towns → camps, oral culture, stone tools, nature-based spirituality
Ancient	Egyptian, Greek, Roman, Mesopotamian, Chinese, Mesoamerican	Monumental architecture, empire systems, mythology entwined with state, limited literacy
Medieval / Feudal	Early, High, Late medieval	Castles, guilds, serfs, church power, low literacy, barter economies
Renaissance / Early Modern	Exploration era, colonial, enlightenment	Printing press, trade ports, rising merchant class, scientific curiosity
Industrial Revolution	18th–19th c. Europe/Americas	Steam tech, factories, class divide, dense urban growth, smog, reform movements
Modern Day	1990–present	Globalization, digital tech, mixed architecture, democracy/consumerism tension
Near Future	+10–50 yrs	Smart cities, AI integration, surveillance ethics, eco-crisis or utopia
Distant Future	+100–1000 yrs	Interstellar travel, post-scarcity, alien contact, transhumanism
Alternate Timeline	Steampunk, dieselpunk, retro-future	Mix of historical tech aesthetics and divergent history
Fantasy Era	High-magic medieval, low-magic renaissance, modern-magic fusion	Alters physics/economy (magic guilds replace industry, etc.)
Post-Apocalyptic	Nuclear, plague, climate collapse	Scarcity economies, fortified communities, distrustful cultures

Determine Geographic Setting / Environment
Choose the physical environment. Geography influences climate, available resources, architecture, economy, and social routines.

Options and Influences:

Setting	Sub-traits	Influence Notes
Coastal	Port city, fishing village, resort town	Maritime trade, humid climate, seafood diet, seafaring culture
Inland	River-based, crossroads, mountain valley	Agriculture or trade hub, reliance on overland routes
Mountainous	Alpine, volcanic, high plateau	Resource extraction (mining), isolation, sturdy architecture
Forested	Temperate, rainforest, taiga	Wood industries, myths about forest spirits, damp climate
Desert	Oasis, dune sea, rocky steppe	Scarcity mindset, water economy, nomadic routes
Plains / Grassland	Farmland, steppe	Agricultural surplus, open-sky culture, seasonal festivals
Island / Archipelago	Tropical, volcanic, cold island	Maritime dependence, unique dialects, island isolation
Urban Center / Metropolis	Capital, trade hub, industrial	Complex class systems, diverse cultures, advanced infrastructure
Rural Village	Agricultural, pastoral	Tight-knit community, oral history, slower pace
Suburban Area	Modern periphery	Commuter culture, consumer landscapes, hidden tensions
Arctic / Tundra	Polar, sub-polar	Survival engineering, limited daylight, strong community reliance
Swamp / Wetlands	Delta, mangrove, bog	Disease influence, unique flora/fauna, superstition
Extraterrestrial	Planet, moon, station	Artificial ecosystems, isolation, alien interaction

Define Cultural / Regional Flavor
Decide the cultural or regional identity. This affects social norms, aesthetics, cuisine, festivals, architecture, art, and language roots.

Options and Influences:

Region	Influence Notes
North America	Individualism, mixed immigration, suburban sprawl
Western Europe	Historical layering, preservation vs. modernity
Eastern Europe	Folklore, resilience, post-imperial complexity
Middle East	Trade crossroads, spiritual centrality, desert architecture
North Africa	Desert-coastal mix, strong oral and market cultures
Sub-Saharan Africa	Clan ties, community focus, musical richness
South Asia	Dense population, spirituality, caste/class dynamics
East Asia	Harmony vs. technology tension, urban density
Southeast Asia	Maritime trade blend, tropical climates
Oceania / Pacific	Islander navigation culture, colonial impacts
Hybrid Culture	Mixed aesthetics, food fusion, layered languages
Fictional Region	Custom synthesis of chosen influences

Define Population Scale
Determines complexity of governance, infrastructure, institutions, and social interactions.

Options and Infrastructure Effects:

Scale	Population	Traits / Infrastructure
Hamlet	<200	Everyone knows everyone; single road; minimal services, 1 store, no hospital
Small Town	200–5,000	Local economy, folk traditions; 1 school, small clinic, 1 police post
Large Town	5,000–20,000	Growing diversity; 2 schools, local paper, small businesses
Small City	20,000–100,000	Defined districts; hospital, multiple schools, police/fire dept
Medium City	100,000–500,000	Subcultures, public transport; malls, colleges, media outlets
Metropolis	500,000–5M	Dense, vertical, multicultural; transit system, multiple universities
Mega-City	>5M	Overcrowded, stratified; massive bureaucracy, sprawling suburbs
Nomadic / Distributed	Mobile populations	No fixed center; mobile infrastructure, trade networks

Determine Societal Development / Technology Level
Determines occupation types, energy, infrastructure sophistication, and communication methods.

Options and Influences:

Level	Traits / Examples	Influence Notes
Pre-Agrarian	Hunter-gatherer, oral tradition	No permanent architecture layer
Agrarian	Farming villages, simple trade	Seasonal rhythms, temples, basic market
Industrial	Steam, factories	Pollution, class struggle, urbanization
Modern	Electricity, cars, internet	Bureaucracy, digital divide, media
Near-Future	Automation, biotech	Smart infrastructure, AI integration
Futuristic / Spacefaring	Advanced AI, FTL travel	Post-scarcity politics, interstellar trade
Magical Tech	Arcane industry, guilds	Energy = mana, institutions = magical guilds

Set Tone / Genre Filter
Determines narrative style, aesthetics, emotional weight, and thematic tension.

Options and Influences:

Genre	Influence Notes
Realistic Slice of Life	Grounded detail, focus on daily routines
Mystery / Thriller	Secrecy, tension, hidden agendas
Romance / Drama	Social networks, emotional tone
Political / Crime	Hierarchies, corruption, intrigue
Fantasy Adventure	Heroic tension, exotic fauna/flora, quest structure
Science Fiction	Tech ethics, discovery, futuristic dilemmas
Horror / Gothic	Isolation, decay, superstition, fear
Satire / Comedy	Exaggeration, archetypes, irony
Surreal / Experimental	Dream logic, symbolic architecture
Post-Apocalyptic	Scarcity, survival economy, distrust
Mythic / Legendary	Archetypes, divine interference, legend shaping

Assign Key Descriptor Tags (Optional but Recommended)
Free-form modifiers that further tune social, economic, cultural, and environmental probabilities in later steps.

Examples:

Economic: wealthy, poor, trade hub, mining economy, tech startup scene

Political: democratic, authoritarian, theocratic, anarchic

Cultural: superstitious, artistic, communal, hierarchical

Environmental: storm-prone, drought-ridden, biodiverse, radioactive

Social Tone: hopeful, cynical, nostalgic, paranoid

Note: Tags act as weighting modifiers — e.g., a “storm-prone coastal town” increases the probability of emergency services and resilient infrastructure appearing.

Step 1 Output

Produce a concise summary of the world/town including:

Era / Time Period

Geographic Setting

Cultural / Regional Flavor

Population Scale

Technology / Societal Level

Tone / Genre

Key Descriptor Tags

Also include derived influences, i.e., notes on how the choices affect infrastructure, institutions, social dynamics, or routines.

Example:

World Scope Summary:
Era: Near Future (AI integration)
Setting: Mountainous Inland Valley
Region: East Asia
Population: Small City (~75,000)
Technology Level: Near-Future (smart infrastructure)
Tone: Mystery/Thriller
Tags: Seismically active zone, high surveillance, tech universities, ancient temples nearby
Derived Influences:
- Frequent tremors → reinforced architecture
- Surveillance → low street crime, high tension
- Academic hub → transient population, youth culture

Step 2 – Generate Infrastructure
Objective: Create the core institutions that define the town/world. Scale them based on population size and type.
Actions:
1.	Governance: mayor’s office, council, or equivalent local authority.
2.	Safety: police, fire, or volunteer emergency services.
3.	Health: clinics, hospitals, pharmacies, or basic medical support.
4.	Education: schools, libraries, and community learning spaces.
5.	Commerce: markets, banks, small businesses, or trade hubs.
6.	Leisure: parks, cafes, theaters, or public gathering areas.
7.	Housing: neighborhood layouts, apartment clusters, family homes.
Output:
•	List of all institutions present, with notes on scale and functionality.
•	Include informal structures if formal institutions are absent (e.g., volunteer watch, traveling medics).
________________________________________
Step 3 – Populate Roles
Objective: Assign placeholder roles to each institution, ensuring the town/world has functional social structure.
Actions:
•	For each institution, define the necessary roles (examples):
o	Clinic → 3 doctors, 6 nurses, 2 admin staff
o	School → 10 teachers, 1 principal
o	Bank → 1 manager, 3 tellers, 1 security guard
o	Lab → 1 head, 2 assistants
o	Shop → 1–2 staff
•	For small towns, allow overlap of roles (e.g., a teacher may also manage farm duties).
Output:
•	Table or list of institutions with associated roles.
•	Note role overlaps if town size is small.
________________________________________
Step 4 – Generate Inhabitants
Objective: Make the town/world “alive” by creating named inhabitants with short bios, quirks, routines, and relationships.
Actions:
1.	For each role or family, assign:
o	Name
o	Age (if relevant)
o	Bio: profession, personality traits, notable characteristics
o	Quirks: small habits or behaviors that add realism
o	Daily routines: activities or responsibilities
2.	Define relationships and social links naturally:
o	Family connections
o	Friendships or rivalries
o	Mentor/student networks
o	Connections influenced by institutions (e.g., children in same school, siblings working at same shop)
3.	Include Pokémon, animals, or other creatures if relevant to the setting.
Output:
•	List or table of inhabitants with key information.
•	Optional narrative notes on town dynamics and interactions.
________________________________________
Usage Notes
•	This framework is repeatable and scalable: it works for small towns, large cities, or fictional worlds.
•	Focus on logical consistency: inhabitants and institutions should make sense for the scope and era.
•	Optional Step 5 (Spatial Layout) can be done later, based on the results of Steps 1–4.
•	The framework can be used for analysis of existing fictional worlds or generation of original worlds.
